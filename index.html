The Fall and Rise of Drug Crimes in San Francisco

In 2008 the SFPD filed 8,405 drug offense reports. By 2021 that number had fallen to 942 representing an 89% collapse over 13 years. Then, suddenly, it turned around. This is the story of what the data shows, and what it can't.

San Francisco Police Department incident data  ·  2003–2025

Introduction Annual Trend The Fall The Rise Conclusion References

Between 2003 and 2025, the San Francisco Police Department recorded more than 950,000 incidents across ten categories of crime. For this data story, we focus on one of those categories: drug offenses.

The dataset combines two SFPD incident report systems: the historical archive covering 2003 to May 2018, and the current reporting system from 2018 onward. Each row is a single reported incident with a date, time, police district, and category. A drug offense means a police officer filed a report, which reflects enforcement activity as much as it reflects actual drug use or dealing. This distinction matters, and we will return to it.

This rise and fall is not spread evenly across the city. In our analysis, one neighborhood stands out: the Tenderloin neighborhood. A dense, historically low-income district north of City Hall, the Tenderloin accounts for about a third of all drug offense reports across the entire 22-year span. The story of drug offenses in San Francisco is, largely, the story of one neighborhood.

But what drives these swings? The data alone cannot tell us. A drop in reported offenses might mean less drug activity. It can also mean fewer arrests, a shift in enforcement priorities, or a policy change that reclassified certain offenses. California's Proposition 47 (passed in 2014), the COVID-19 pandemic, and San Francisco's fentanyl crisis all leave fingerprints in the data. The data cannot tell us which fingerprint belongs to which hand.

Act I

A City's Habit

The annual count of drug offense reports tells a story in four distinct eras. Scroll to explore each one.

2003–2008 · Peak Era

Eight thousand reports a year

Drug offense reporting started high and kept climbing. In 2008, SFPD filed 8,405 reports — the highest single-year total in the dataset. Drug offenses were a dominant in the city's enforcement landscape, and concentrated in a handful of districts and kept rising year over year.

2009–2019 · Long Decline

A thirteen-year slide

After the 2008 peak, reported drug offenses fell almost every year for more than a decade. Enforcement priorities shifted. California policy changed. Cannabis decriminalization reduced a major category of arrests. The decline was steady and driven by forces that go beyond the data itself.

2020–2021 · COVID Trough

Nearly to zero

The pandemic accelerated the collapse. Street-level enforcement contracted sharply, which was a pattern documented statewide, with California arrests falling by roughly 40% in 2020 [9]. In 2021, SFPD filed just 942 drug offense reports, an 89% drop from the 2008 peak. By this measure, drug crime had nearly vanished from San Francisco. Unfortunately, it hadn't.

2022–2025 · The Rebound

A sharp reversal

Starting in 2022, drug offense reports surged. By 2025, SFPD filed 4,451 reports representing a nearly five fold increase from the historical low in 2021. Drug offenses climbed from 2.5% of selected crime categories in 2021 to 23% by late 2025, overtaking assault as the most reported category in this group.

Policy · November 2014

Proposition 47 changes the rules

California's Proposition 47 downgraded simple drug possession from a felony to a misdemeanor. According to PPIC, arrests for drug and property crimes fell after those offenses were reclassified making Prop 47 a plausible contributor to San Francisco's long and steady decline [4].

Policy · 2006–2016

Cannabis drops off the radar

A large US study found cannabis decriminalization cut possession arrest rates 40%–80% during the 2010s [5]. San Francisco reinforced this in 2006, making adult marijuana enforcement its lowest law enforcement priority [6].

Geography · 2003–2021

Everything funnels into the Tenderloin

As citywide incidents fell, the remaining activity became more concentrated in the Tenderloin neighborhood. By 2021, it accounted for 55% of all drug offense reports, up from 27% in 2003. The collapse was uneven: as the city cooled, one neighborhood stayed hot [7].

Act III

The Rise

Around 2022, drug offense reports reversed sharply. From 2.5% of selected crimes in 2021 to 23% by late 2025, drug offenses overtook assault as the most reported category in this group.

Context · 2019–2022

The fentanyl crisis arrives

San Francisco is dealing with an opioid epidemic. Fentanyl and other synthetic opioids surged rapidly from 2019 onward, and are significantly cheaper in San Francisco compared to other US cities [1].

From 2018 to 2020, drug overdose deaths in San Francisco almost tripled — from 259 to 726 — with most attributed to fentanyl [2].

Response · 2019–2023

A billion dollars for intervention

Between 2019 and 2023, California spent around $1 billion to address the crisis, roughly half from the state's general fund and half from the federal government. These efforts focused on prevention, treatment, and medication-assisted care [8].

Enforcement · November 2023

All Hands on Deck

In November 2023, the "All Hands on Deck" initiative brought together local, state, and federal agents to tackle the epidemic in San Francisco's Tenderloin neighborhood [3].

The surge in reported drug offenses since 2022 reflects both this enforcement intensity and the underlying crisis. The data cannot tell us which force is driving the number higher.

Conclusion

Which fingerprint, which hand?

The data presents a clear pattern — a long fall followed by a sharp rise — but it resists a single clean explanation. The same movement in a crime report count can mean many different things: more policing, less policing; more drug use, less drug use; a new law, an old habit. The numbers are real, but they mirror enforcement decisions just as much as they mirror underlying criminal activity.

What the data does show clearly is geography. The Tenderloin area has been at the center of this story for more than two decades. As drug reports fell citywide, they fell slowest there. As they rose again, they rose fastest there. Any meaningful response to San Francisco's drug crisis will have to investigate what has made the Tenderloin neighborhood its persistent epicenter.

Do the 2025 numbers reflect progress, or just more policing? The fingerprints are there, but matching them to the right hand remains the harder task.
