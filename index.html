The Fall and Rise of Drug Crimes in San Francisco

Between 2003 and 2025, drug offense reports swung from 8,400 a year to fewer than 1,000 — then climbed back to 4,400. This is the story of what the numbers show, and what they can't.

Between 2003 and 2025, the San Francisco Police Department recorded more than 950,000 incidents across ten categories of crime. For this data story, we focus on one of those categories: drug offenses.

The dataset combines two SFPD incident report systems: the historical archive covering 2003 to May 2018, and the current reporting system from 2018 onward. Each row is a single reported incident, with a date, time, police district, and category. A drug offense in this dataset means a police officer filed a report, which reflects enforcement activity as much as it reflects actual drug use or dealing. This is an important distinction to note, and one we will return to.

The data shows an interesting pattern: a long decline followed by a sharp rebound. In 2008, SFPD logged 8,405 drug offense reports — the highest in the dataset. Over the next thirteen years, that number continued to fall, bottoming out at just 942 in 2021, a drop of nearly 89%. Then it turned around. By 2025, drug reports had climbed back to 4,451, nearly five times the 2021 low.

This rise and fall is not spread evenly across the city. In our analysis, one neighborhood stands out: the Tenderloin. The Tenderloin is a dense, historically low-income district north of City Hall. Across the entire 22-year span, the Tenderloin area accounts for roughly a third of all drug offense reports in San Francisco. The story of drug offenses in SF is, in large part, the story of one neighborhood.

But what drives these swings? The data alone cannot tell us. A drop in reported drug offenses might mean less drug activity — or it might mean fewer arrests, a shift in enforcement priorities, or a policy change that reclassified certain offenses. California's Proposition 47 (passed in November 2014), which downgraded simple drug possession from a felony to a misdemeanor, almost certainly shaped the numbers in the years that followed. The COVID-19 pandemic upended enforcement in 2020 and 2021. And San Francisco's ongoing fentanyl crisis has drawn intense political pressure since 2022. All of these forces leave fingerprints in the data — but the data cannot tell us which fingerprint belongs to which hand.

Act I

A City's Habit

The annual count of drug offense reports tells a story in three distinct eras. Each one reflects a different set of forces acting on the city.

2003–2008 · Peak Era

Eight thousand reports a year

Drug offense reporting started high and kept climbing. In 2008, SFPD filed 8,405 reports — the highest single-year total in the dataset. At this point, drug offenses were a dominant feature of the city's enforcement landscape, reflecting both the scale of street-level activity and the priorities of the department.

2009–2019 · Long Decline

A thirteen-year slide

After the 2008 peak, reported drug offenses fell almost every year for more than a decade. Enforcement priorities shifted. California policy changed. Cannabis decriminalization reduced a large category of arrests nationwide. The decline was steady and deep — but its causes were layered.

2020–2021 · COVID Trough

Nearly to zero

The pandemic accelerated the collapse. Street-level enforcement contracted sharply. In 2021, SFPD filed just 942 drug offense reports — an 89% drop from the 2008 peak. By the measure of this data, drug crime had nearly vanished from the city. It hadn't. It was about to return.

Act II

The Fall

From 7,468 incidents in 2003 to 913 in 2021. Several forces help explain the collapse — but none tells the whole story on its own.

Policy · November 2014

Proposition 47 changes the rules

California's Proposition 47 downgraded simple drug possession from a felony to a misdemeanor. According to PPIC, arrests for drug and property crimes fell after those offenses were reclassified, making Prop 47 a plausible contributor to San Francisco's drop in recorded drug offense incidents.

PPIC is careful not to overclaim: it finds no evidence that changes in drug arrests after Prop 47 led to increases in crime, but the data do not show whether substance use or addiction changed. [4]

Policy · 2006–2016

Cannabis drops off the radar

A large US study found that cannabis decriminalization was associated with 40%–80% reductions in possession arrest rates during the 2010s. [5]

San Francisco reinforced that shift in 2006, making adult marijuana enforcement its lowest law-enforcement priority — covering investigation, citation, arrest, and property seizure, with exceptions for sales to minors and public sales. [6]

Geography · 2003–2021

Everything funnels into the Tenderloin

As citywide incidents fell, the remaining activity became more concentrated in the Tenderloin. By 2021, the Tenderloin accounted for 55% of all drug offense reports — up from 27% in 2003.

The collapse was uneven: the city quieted, but one neighborhood stayed hot. This is consistent with later reporting and city data showing overdoses and drug incidents remained heavily concentrated in the Tenderloin even as citywide totals fell. [7]

Act III

The Rise

Around 2022, drug offense reports reversed sharply. From 2.5% of selected crimes in 2021 to 23% by late 2025 — drug offenses overtook assault as the most reported category in this group.

Context · 2019–2022

The fentanyl crisis arrives

San Francisco is dealing with an opioid epidemic. Fentanyl and other synthetic opioids gained ground rapidly from 2019 onward, and are significantly cheaper in San Francisco compared to other US cities. [1]

From 2018 to 2020, drug overdose deaths in San Francisco almost tripled — from 259 to 726 — with most attributed to fentanyl. [2]

Response · 2019–2023

A billion dollars for intervention

Between 2019 and 2023, California spent around $1 billion to address the crisis — roughly half from the state's general fund, half from the federal government. These efforts focused on prevention, treatment, and medication-assisted care. [8]

Enforcement · November 2023

All Hands on Deck

In November 2023, the "All Hands on Deck" initiative brought together local, state, and federal agents to tackle the drug epidemic — focused on the Tenderloin. [3]

The surge in reported drug offenses since 2022 reflects both this enforcement intensity and the underlying crisis that produced it. Which factor drives the numbers more is, again, a question the data cannot answer.

Conclusion

Which fingerprint, which hand?

The data presents a clear pattern — a long fall, a sharp rise — but it resists a single clean explanation. The same movement in a crime report count can mean many different things: more police, fewer police; more drug use, less drug use; a new law, an old habit. The numbers are real, but they mirror enforcement decisions as much as they mirror underlying activity.

What the data does show clearly is geography. The Tenderloin has been at the center of this story for more than two decades. As drug reports fell citywide, they fell slowest there. As they rose again, they rose fastest there. Any durable response to San Francisco's drug crisis will have to reckon with what has made the Tenderloin its persistent epicenter.

The fingerprints are in the data. Matching them to a hand remains the harder task.
